## PART A — THEORY OF CHANGE

### 1. The problem we are addressing

Financial education has been statutory in English secondary schools since 2014 (within Mathematics and Citizenship), yet delivery remains inconsistent and outcomes are weak:

- A large proportion of young people do not recall receiving financial education at school, and many report leaving school without the confidence to make everyday financial decisions. *(Cite: MaPS / Young Money / YBS "Saving Generation Z" research — [insert specific figures].)*
- The core weakness identified by MaPS is the **gap between classroom theory and real-world behaviour** — knowledge taught in the abstract does not reliably transfer into habits at home.
- Most existing provision teaches money through **hypothetical scenarios** ("imagine you are saving for…"), which are necessarily decontextualised from the learner's own financial life.

**The need:** a way to deliver financial education that is anchored in a young person's *actual* financial experience, at the moment a concept becomes relevant to them — closing the theory-to-behaviour gap.

### 2. Our hypothesis (the mechanism of change)

Morechard is built on a single pedagogical hypothesis, grounded in **experiential and situated learning theory**:

> *Financial concepts taught at the moment a learner encounters them in their own real activity — earning, saving, spending, borrowing — are understood more deeply and retained and applied more reliably than the same concepts taught abstractly.*

Morechard operationalises this. As a child tracks real chores, earnings, goals and purchases, the app detects behavioural triggers and delivers the single most relevant lesson at that teachable moment, personalised with the child's own figures (e.g. their actual median chore rate, their actual balance). The activity-tracking exists to *generate the teachable moment*; the education is the purpose.

### 3. Logic model

| Stage | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Inputs** | The Learning Lab curriculum (structured modules across the financial-literacy pillars: Earning & Value, Spending & Choices, Saving & Growth, Borrowing & Debt, Investing & Future, Society & Wellbeing); a behavioural-trigger engine; the child's own (anonymous) activity data; curriculum mapped to FCA Financial Capability / National Curriculum (Citizenship & Maths). |
| **Activities** | A child uses Morechard to track real chores, goals and spending. Behavioural triggers fire modules at relevant moments. Each module follows a four-part structure: **Hook** (real-data, personalised), **Lesson** (plain-English concept), **Lab** (applied numeracy using the child's own figures + reflection), **Quiz** (comprehension check with pass threshold). |
| **Outputs** | Modules delivered and completed; quizzes attempted and passed; numeracy Labs completed using real personal data; reflection tasks engaged. |
| **Short-term outcomes (during/just after use)** | Increased financial **knowledge** (concepts understood); increased financial **confidence**; ability to apply numeracy to a personal financial situation. |
| **Medium-term outcomes (weeks–months)** | Changed financial **behaviour** in-app (e.g. increased saving consistency, more deliberate spending, use of the 24-hour reflection on large purchases); improved ability to evaluate financial choices (opportunity cost, borrowing decisions). |
| **Long-term outcomes / impact** | Improved real-world financial capability and habits carried beyond the app; young people better equipped to navigate adult financial products (credit, BNPL, savings, speculative assets) they will encounter. |

### 4. Key assumptions (and how we mitigate the risk if they fail)

| Assumption | Risk if false | Mitigation / test |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Real-data triggering improves engagement and retention vs abstract teaching | The core hypothesis fails | This is the primary thing the evaluation tests (see Part B). |
| Children use the app at home with real activity | Triggers don't fire; lessons become a static library | Pilot design must drive home engagement; we measure activation/usage. |
| Content is pitched at the right level for the age tier | Lessons too hard/easy; disengagement | Pre/post comprehension and pupil feedback; differentiation work (see accessibility plan). |
| A parent/carer supports app use at home | Lower engagement | Modules and labs are written to work with minimal adult involvement; family-neutral by design. |

### 5. Alignment to national standards

The curriculum is explicitly mapped to:
- **MaPS UK Strategy for Financial Wellbeing** — children & young people financial-capability outcomes.
- **National Curriculum (England):** Citizenship (financial decision-making, money management) and Mathematics (the integrated numeracy Labs).
- **FCA Financial Capability** principles (value for money, buffer/rainy-day saving, consumer rights).
- International parity for other locales: Jump$tart / Council for Economic Education (US); National Strategy for Financial Education (Poland).

## PART B — EVALUATION PLAN

### 1. Evaluation questions

1. Does using Morechard increase young people's **financial knowledge** (concepts taught in the modules)?
2. Does it increase their **financial confidence and attitudes**?
3. Does it change observable **financial behaviour** (in-app, and self-reported at home)?
4. Is the **real-data, triggered** delivery more engaging and effective than learners' prior experience of financial education? *(Differentiation question — the core hypothesis.)*
5. Is the resource **usable and accessible** across the ability range (including SEND/EAL/lower reading age)?

### 2. Outcomes, indicators and instruments

| Outcome | Indicator | Instrument |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Knowledge gain | Change in score on a financial-concept assessment | **Pre/post knowledge quiz** ([10–15] items mapped to module concepts) |
| Confidence/attitudes | Change in self-rated confidence and money attitudes | **Validated self-report scale** — [e.g. adapt MaPS financial-capability survey items for young people] |
| Behaviour change | Saving consistency, deliberate spending, reflection-tool use | **In-app behavioural data** (anonymous), pre/post comparison |
| Engagement | Module completion, quiz pass rates, voluntary return use | **App analytics** + pupil feedback |
| Relevance vs prior provision | Pupil-reported comparison; teacher judgement | **Pupil survey + teacher interview/questionnaire** |
| Usability & accessibility | Completion and comprehension across ability bands | **Teacher observation + differentiated feedback capture** |

### 6. Ethics, safeguarding and data protection

- **No children's personal data is collected.** Morechard does not store names, emails, addresses or dates of birth; accounts are paired via anonymous parent-generated tokens. GDPR and GDPR-K compliant by design.
- Evaluation data is anonymous/aggregate; no pupil is identifiable.
- Consent obtained via the school/parents per [school's process]. Right to withdraw respected.
- All content is non-diagnostic; modules touching wellbeing (e.g. Money & Mental Health) include signposting to YoungMinds, Mind and Samaritans.

### 7. Limitations (stated honestly)

- A single-school pilot limits generalisability; results are indicative, to be strengthened by subsequent cohorts.
- A self-selected school may not represent all settings.
- Short follow-up window limits claims about long-term behaviour change; [follow-up check] partially addresses this.

### 8. Outputs — the Quality Mark evidence portfolio

This evaluation is designed to produce: a baseline/endpoint dataset, a pilot evaluation report, pupil and teacher feedback, and a usability/accessibility summary — together forming the evidence portfolio for the Financial Education Quality Mark.
